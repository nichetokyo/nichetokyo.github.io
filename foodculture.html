Food Culture

Links to other important things I wrote down that you should look at before asking me about.

  • People in Tokyo eat out a lot.
  • Meal times usually go from 11am-2pm for lunch, and dinner starting from 6pm-8pm running until 9-11pm.
  • Tipping is not a practice in Japan. Some places will have cover charges or a built-in service charge.
  • Some restaurants you need to pay at the front of the restaurant, rather than giving the bill to the waiter.

Food Classification:

There are many ways to classify food, whether by type (I.e. noodles, sushi) or by meal. Tokyo eateries have a range of options, catering most of the favourites. There will also always be a restaurant serving what you’re looking for at any odd hour. However, knowing which foods are found where, and when, will help you hedge on better experiences.

For lack of ba better way of classifying it, I think of Japanese as three types of food:

  • street food which is fast food, economical dishes, and festival foods
  • home food (can overlap with street / high food) usually simple, yummy dishes.
  • high food or proper sit-down places
  • noodles because they’re so important to me, they deserve their own category
  • Sushi also needs its own category because there are 100x more options for raw fish items in Japan

Caveat: Much of “high food” has been inspired by street food. Everything you can find in the street food category you can find in a 5-star restaurant.

Breakfast

Breakfast is often eaten at home or on the go. However, Japanese restaurant chains will always have traditional Japanese breakfast specials, which are usually rice or rice-poridge based. Another typical breakfast are buns!

Lunch

Lunches are commonly bentos, noodles (soba, udon, ramen), set meals (teishoku), rice bowls, sushi, and an equal number of Western fare (I.e. Italian).

These can come from department stores, restaurants specializing in a specific type of food, Kissaten, Japanese restaurant chains, and Western restaurants.

Dinner

Dinners or evening meals can include sushi, noodles, Japanese fast food chains, pubs (izakaya), hot pot (sukiyaki), teppanyaki (grilled meats).

Below, I have a list of some favourites that people want to try in Japan as well as some commonly found local foods. Note that many street food items also have restaurants specializing in them.

Street food & Festival Food

My only note is that many noodle joints fall into this category. Ramen stalls certainly do. So do the ubiquitous noodle stands in train stations. You can find both of these in the noodles category below.

Tempura (天ぷら)

Deep-fried vegetables, shrimp, or any other seafood. The way to judge quality is the thinness of the encasing dough – paper thin. Fresh oil also has a much lighter, cleaner flavour that accentuates the just-cooked ingredient inside. Dip into the sauce provided before taking a bite, and a bite of rice to go with.

Tonkatsu (とんかつ)

Breaded pork cutlets. They usually come with a brown sauce, but sometimes other sauces like curry or a shop’s specialty. Often comes with a wedge of lemon and a side of cabbage or salad.

Yakitori (やきとり、焼き鳥)

Grilled chicken on skewers. It’s one of the grilled items category called kushiyaki. You usually need to decide on salt (shio) or.sweet-soy sauce (dare). Usually a skewer starts at ¥150, but can quickly go up.

Often found in: street stalls (Shinjuku), festivals, izakayas, and go well with beers.

Okonomiyaki (お好み焼き)

A grilled, eggy-pancake topped with lots of lettuce, and whatever else is ordered or offered.

There are two main styles: Osaka and Hiroshima. The Hiroshima one has noodles, is much less pancakey, and has a thin crust, usually with an egg on one side.

The classic is usually lettuce, pork, green onions, usually slathered in mayonnaise and okonomiyaki sauce. Other popular ingredients include ginger, corn, beef and tuna depending on the concoction.

Often Found in: specialty shops, street festivals, street stalls

Monja

Tokyo’s version of Okonomiyaki is Monja, found at Tsukishima (with the corresponding station name).

It’s often made without eggs and many usually have a fish as a topping.

Takoyaki (たこ焼き)

These are grilled octopus balls made on a pan that resembles a waffle maker. The batter and fillings are poured in, and expertly flipped using a toothpick. Be careful, the filling is always mouth-searing.

Sukiyaki (すき焼き)

Sukiyaki is beef served with a variety of vegetables in a cast iron. They come in small, individual sizes and larger shared pots.

Yakisoba 焼きそば

Fried ramen-style noodles. Typically spotted at food stalls during festivals and outdoor events. May also be found in sandwiches or with ramen replaced by udon noodles, which becomes yakiudon.

Home food

I just throw these into home foods because in some ways they’re comfort foods people associate with mom’s cooking. Virtually all of these home foods can be found in restaurants, modern or more traditional.

Curry-Rice (カレーライス)

Japan has a rather unique curry culture that is relatively different from India’s. The curry tends to be thicker (and occasionally sweeter) and comes on top of Japanese rice, not Thai or basmati. There’s usually crunchy red pickled cucumber and radish (fukujinzuke, 福神漬) as a relish.

Omu-Rice (オムライス)

Another Japanese favorite is to wrap rice with a thin layer of lightly fried egg. The kind of Omu-rice (omelette + rice) that I like has a ketchup or tomato-sauce flavor.

Oden (おでん)

Soup stock with daikon, konbu, egg, and fish cakes, and konnyaku usually, and some other things.You can find these in 7-Eleven when the weather gets colder.

High food

Noodles

Soba そば、蕎

Buckwheat noodles, more or less gray in color. The Japanese consider them to be more of a lightweight snack material rather than a meal. Can be served hot or cold. Soba served cold comes with dipping sauce (tsuyu).

Protip 1: Soba just means noodles. Don’t be thrown off if a ramen shop says “soba” instead of ramen.

Protip 2: Ask or look for soba-yu, which is the water the soba is boiled in, to pour into your dipping sauce. Doing so waters down the saltiness of the dipping sauce and makes a nice drinkable soup.

Udon うどん

Thicker wheat noodles. Typically served warm, though can be appreciated cold as with soba. Sanuki udon is a variant popular in the southern prefecture of Kagawa, with a distinctly chewy consistency.

Somen そうめん、素麺

Super thin wheat noodles - more popular towards the southern part of the country. Typically eaten cold. Nagashi-somen is a fun deviation where restaurants serve somen on bamboo flutes with running water, and you pluck somen from it as they travel downward.

Tsukemen つけ麺

Tsukemen isn’t so much a type of noodle as it is a way to consume it – by dipping warm or cold noodles into a broth. The kinds I’ve often had were ramen noodles with a thick, rich broth.

Hiyashi-chuka 冷やし中華

A summer staple, hiyashi-chuka is cold ramen noodles with garnishes (typically sliced cucumber, ham, egg) dribbled over with a semi-sweet watery, soy-based sauce.

Ramen ラーメン

  • Shio 塩、しお
    Salt-based soup, pale, yellowish. Usually with a lot of vegetables.

  • Shoyu, 醤油、しょうゆ
    Soy-based soup, darker brown.

  • Tonkotsu, 豚骨、とんこつ
    Pork bone broth, milky brown. Richer, buttery flavor. Sometimes referred to as “Hakata ramen”, from the region where it was first popular.

  • Miso, 味噌、みそ
    Miso-based soup. Richer in flavor - a northern favorite.

  • Any other variety and soup base you can imagine (cheese, chicken broth, shrimp broth, tomato-based soup, vegetarian & vegan)

Sushi

Sushi comes in a lot of forms and vary from region to region (based on the seafood available). They all include raw (or mostly raw) seafood and occasionally red meat (like horse).

Sashimi

A slice of fish. No rice.

Chirashizushi

One of my favourite types of sushi. It’s a mix of sashimi on top of a rice bowl / box. It basically is the odds and ends of sashimi pieces (in cheaper places) or can be very elegantly arranged (department stores).

Makizushi

Fish wrapped in rice and seaweed. They come as thin rolls. These are the more take-away style types of sushi. Usually they’re single flavour, or there’s an egg-mix one. Please don’t look for a California roll – you might find it in an American-Japanese specialty restaurant if you’re lucky.

Kaitenzushi

This is sushi served on a conveyor belt at a bar. they’re not special dishes. YOU PICK THE each plate the sushi is on is usually color-coded by price so that you can tell if a particular sushi is cheaper or more expensive than you’d like. Kaitenzushi is often considered a lower quality sushi-eating experience by locals.

Nigiri(zushi)

A slice of fish on top of a ball of rice. At restaurants, this is more the standard.

Temaki

I have never seen temaki in Japan.
